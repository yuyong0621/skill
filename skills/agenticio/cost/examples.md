# Cost — Real Usage Examples

*Copy these prompts directly into your AI agent to get started.*

---

## Quick Start Prompts

### Get Cost Clarity

"I run a service business with $800,000 in revenue and I feel like I should be more profitable than I am. Help me figure out where the money is going."

### Product Profitability

"I sell three products but I suspect one of them is actually losing money. How do I figure out the true cost of each one?"

### Cost Reduction

"I need to cut $200,000 from my cost base without cutting the capabilities that drive revenue. Where do I start?"

### Pricing Confidence

"I have been pricing based on gut feel and competitor comparison. Help me build pricing based on what things actually cost me."

## Conversation Examples

### Example 1: Margin Mystery

User: My revenue grew 25% last year but my profit barely moved. I do not understand what happened.

Agent: Revenue growing faster than profit almost always means costs grew faster than revenue — or that the revenue mix shifted toward lower-margin products or customers. Let us find which one. Tell me your total revenue and total cost of goods sold for last year versus the year before, and whether the 25% growth came from more customers, higher prices, or more volume from existing customers. The answer to those questions will tell us whether this is a cost structure problem, a pricing problem, or a customer mix problem — and each of those has a different solution.

---

### Example 2: True Product Cost

User: I make and sell furniture. I know what materials cost but I am not sure I am pricing correctly.

Agent: Materials are only part of your cost. To price correctly you need fully-loaded cost per piece, which includes: direct materials, direct labor time at your fully-loaded labor rate including benefits and payroll taxes, the portion of your facility cost attributable to the space and time the piece occupies, equipment depreciation, and a proportional share of overhead — management time, insurance, utilities, sales and marketing. Most furniture makers who feel underpaid are not charging for their labor at a real rate or are not allocating overhead. Tell me your top three products by volume and I will help you build a true cost model for each one.

---

### Example 3: SaaS Cost Sprawl

User: I run a 12-person company and I feel like our software subscriptions have gotten out of control. We are probably paying for things nobody uses.

Agent: Software subscription sprawl is one of the most common and most recoverable cost problems in small businesses. The audit process is straightforward. Pull every recurring charge from your credit card and bank statements for the past three months. List every software tool with its monthly cost and who uses it. For each tool, ask two questions: could we function without it, and is there a cheaper alternative that does 80% of what this does. Most companies that do this audit find 20 to 30 percent of their software spend is on tools with duplicative functionality or tools that the team has organically stopped using. Tell me your approximate monthly software spend and I will help you build the audit framework.

---

### Example 4: Hiring Cost Impact

User: I am thinking about hiring two more people. Revenue is growing but I am nervous about the impact on my cost structure.

Agent: The true cost of a new hire is typically 1.25 to 1.4 times their base salary when you add payroll taxes, benefits, equipment, software licenses, management time, and onboarding. So two hires at $70,000 each cost the business $175,000 to $196,000 per year all-in, not $140,000. The question is what revenue or margin those hires enable that would not otherwise be possible. If they are revenue-generating roles, what is the realistic revenue contribution in year one and year two. If they are operational roles, what cost or capacity constraint do they relieve and what is that worth. Run me through what these two roles would do and I will help you model the cost-benefit.
